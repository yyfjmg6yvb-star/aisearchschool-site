How local businesses can appear in Google AI Overviews in 2026

Chris · June 2026 · 7 min read

Google AI Overviews now appear on roughly 15% of all Google searches — and that number keeps climbing. When one appears, it compresses what used to be ten blue links into a single synthesised paragraph with two or three cited sources. The businesses that get cited get visibility. The businesses that don't lose traffic — even if they'd been ranking at position one for years. Here's how to be one of the cited ones.

What Google AI Overviews actually are

Picture a knowledgeable assistant who reads all the top search results, pulls out the most useful bits, and summarises them for you in a box at the top of the page. That's an AI Overview. Google's AI reads your website, your Google Business Profile, your reviews, and dozens of other sources — then decides whether you're worth citing in its summary.

For local businesses, this is a bigger deal than for e-commerce or content sites. When someone searches "best plumber in Manchester" or "dog groomer near me," an AI Overview might appear and name two or three specific businesses. If your competitor gets named and you don't, they get the call.

Key stat

Google AI Overviews are present on roughly 15% of all Google searches globally in 2026, and the share grows each quarter. Businesses cited in AI Overviews get visibility even when the user doesn't scroll down to organic results.

The five things Google looks at for local AI Overviews

Google's AI pulls from multiple sources when building a local AI Overview. Understanding what those sources are tells you exactly what to work on.

1. Your Google Business Profile

This is the single most important signal for local AI Overviews. If your Google Business Profile is incomplete, outdated, or inactive, you're already at a disadvantage. Google's AI heavily weights how active and complete your profile is.

What "active" means in practice: adding photos at least once a week, posting business updates (not just offers — actual updates like "we just finished a project for a local school" or "new appointment slots just opened"), and responding to every review within 24 hours. Businesses that go 30+ days without a photo or post see measurable drops in AI Overview citations.

2. Your website's structure and clarity

Google's AI doesn't just read your homepage. It tries to understand what you do, who you serve, and where you're based — and it prefers websites that answer these questions clearly and fast. A well-structured services page with specific details ("we cover Manchester, Salford, and the surrounding areas") performs better than vague language ("we serve the local area").

Local schema markup also matters here. Adding LocalBusiness schema to your website tells Google's AI exactly what type of business you are, your address, your phone number, your opening hours, and your service area. Without it, the AI has to guess.

3. Name, address, and phone number consistency

Your business name, address, and phone number need to be identical across every platform where you appear: your website, Google Business Profile, Yelp, Facebook, any industry directories, your Apple Maps listing, and anywhere else your business is mentioned online. Even small differences — "St" vs "Street," old phone numbers that still appear on old directories — reduce your trustworthiness score in Google's AI systems.

This consistency is called NAP consistency, and Google's AI treats it like a fact-check. If your "facts" don't match up across the web, you're less likely to be cited as a reliable source.

4. Review quality and quantity

Reviews are an increasingly important input for local AI Overviews. But it's not just the star rating — it's the content of the reviews. Reviews that mention your location, your specific services, and the outcome you delivered are far more powerful than generic praise.

A review that says "I called Dave on a Tuesday and he fixed our boiler in two hours — haven't had a problem since" is exactly what Google's AI is looking for. It confirms you're a real local business, you fix boilers, and customers are happy. That's three data points in one review.

5. Fresh, specific content on your website

Google's AI increasingly favours content that's been updated recently. An "about us" page that hasn't changed since 2019 is a weaker source than one that was updated this year. This doesn't mean rewriting everything constantly — it means keeping your key pages current: updating your services if they've changed, adding recent project examples, and refreshing your area coverage information.

The practical checklist

  1. Complete every field in your Google Business Profile — including services, products, attributes, and description
  2. Add a new photo to your GBP every week (even just a photo of a job you finished)
  3. Post a GBP update at least once every two weeks
  4. Respond to every review — especially negative ones — within 24 hours
  5. Add LocalBusiness schema to your website with your full address, phone, opening hours, and service area
  6. Audit your NAP consistency — Google your own business name and check that every listing shows identical information
  7. Ask satisfied customers to leave reviews that mention what you did and where
  8. Update your website's services page with specific details about what you offer and which areas you cover

What not to do

A few things that feel like they should help but don't — or actively hurt you:

  • Keyword-stuffing your GBP description. Google's AI is smart enough to spot it, and it looks untrustworthy. Write like a human describing their business.
  • Ignoring negative reviews. An unanswered negative review signals to Google that you don't care about customers. A thoughtful, professional response actually improves your standing.
  • Listing a fake address or service area just to appear in more searches. Google catches this eventually, and it can get your listing suspended entirely.
  • Buying reviews. Fake reviews are not only against Google's policies — they're also easy for AI to detect because they lack the specific, personal detail that real reviews contain.

How quickly will this work?

The GBP changes (adding photos, posting updates, responding to reviews) can start showing effect within weeks — Google's systems update frequently. The website changes (schema, content updates) typically take 4-8 weeks to be fully indexed and weighted. The NAP consistency work is slower — it takes time for old directory listings to update or be removed.

Think of this as a 90-day project rather than a quick fix. Most local businesses that are genuinely active and consistent start seeing AI Overview citations within that window.

Bottom line

Google AI Overviews cite businesses that Google trusts. Trust is built through an active Google Business Profile, consistent information across the web, quality reviews with specific detail, and a clear, structured website. None of this requires any special technical skills — just consistency.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to pay Google to appear in AI Overviews?

No. Google AI Overviews are organic — they're based on Google's assessment of your authority and relevance, not paid placement. You can't buy your way into an AI Overview, but you can earn your way in through the steps above.

My business already ranks well on Google. Will I automatically appear in AI Overviews?

Not necessarily. Traditional SEO rankings and AI Overview citations use overlapping but different signals. A business that ranks #1 organically can still be absent from AI Overviews if their Google Business Profile is thin or their reviews lack detail. It's worth treating AI visibility as a separate project alongside traditional SEO.

How do I know if I'm appearing in Google AI Overviews?

Search for your own services plus your location and see what comes up. Ask a few customers to do the same on their devices (Google personalises results slightly). You can also use Google Search Console to monitor clicks from AI-related features, though Google's reporting on this is still developing.

Does my Google Business Profile category matter?

Yes — significantly. Your primary category tells Google exactly what type of business you are, which determines which local searches you're eligible to appear in. Make sure your primary category is as specific as possible (e.g. "Boiler Installation Service" rather than just "Plumber") and add all relevant secondary categories.

What if I serve customers at their location rather than having a shop front?

You can set up your Google Business Profile as a service-area business without displaying a physical address. The key is to clearly define your service area, add your service area in the GBP settings, and make sure your website also mentions the areas you cover. AI Overviews do include service-area businesses — you just need the same signals as any other local business.
